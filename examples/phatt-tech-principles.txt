PHATT TECH Operating Principles
For use with Loophole adversarial testing

---

## Core Mission
We help small businesses (10-100 employees) run their IT and operations through automation and intelligent agents — replacing traditional managed IT with something faster, smarter, and cheaper.

## Foundational Principles

1. We charge for outcomes and value created, not time spent. If our work creates more value than it costs, the client wins. We win. If a client treats us as hourly labor, we're in the wrong relationship.

2. We only work with clients who have decision-making authority and exercise it. Clients who need to "check with my partner" or "think about it" are not clients we can serve well. Paralysis is a client problem, not ours.

3. We pick our clients. Not every dollar is worth earning. We prefer fewer clients who respect our work and pay on time over volume that drains us.

4. We do not do unpaid work. Proposals, discovery, architecture planning — if it takes real time, it gets priced. We may offer a small initial conversation to determine fit, but that is the limit of free work.

5. Scope is scope. Original agreements are honored. Changes cost money. We do not absorb scope creep because clients forget to budget for it.

6. We do not carry client debt. We do not invoice and wait 60-90 days. ACH preferred. Credit risk is not part of our business model.

7. Our automation work is our intellectual property until it's paid for. Clients can use what we build them; they don't own how we built it until the invoice is settled.

8. We set clear timelines and expect clients to meet them. When a client delays, they consume our capacity without generating value. Delayed clients cost us money.

9. We will not compromise the integrity of the systems we build. Cheap, fragile, or risky solutions that hurt the client long-term are not acceptable because the client demanded them short-term.

10. We will recommend what clients need, not what they can afford. If a client can't afford our recommendation, we help them find what they can afford — but we don't pretend a bad solution is a good one.

11. We compete on automation capability, not on being the cheapest IT provider. We are not competing with the $500/month "we'll handle your servers" shop. Our market is businesses who want to operate at a higher level.

12. We prefer long-term relationships over transactional engagements. We want clients who stay for years, not clients who bounce every time a cheaper option appears.

---

## Client Selection Principles

13. We prefer clients with decision-maker accessibility. If we can't reach the person who makes decisions, we cannot serve them effectively.

14. We avoid clients with a history of payment problems, disputed invoices, or adversarial vendor relationships. This is a strong negative signal we do not ignore.

15. We do not work with clients who view us as replaceable labor. If a client is interviewing three MSPs to get the lowest price, we are not the right fit.

16. We prefer clients whose business depends on their IT working. Businesses for whom IT is mission-critical value our work more than businesses that view IT as overhead.

---

## Automation-First Principles

17. We believe automation should reduce ongoing operational cost for clients, not just add another tool. If our work doesn't make their business cheaper to run within 6 months, we've failed.

18. We prefer solutions that require minimal ongoing human intervention. The best automation is the kind clients forget exists because it just works.

19. We build for reliability over feature count. A client with three automations that never fail is better served than a client with twelve that break quarterly.

20. We prefer tools and platforms that we can support long-term. We do not build on platforms we can't maintain or that lock us into vendors we don't control.

---

## Business Conduct Principles

21. We do not badmouth competitors or try to scare clients into working with us. We explain what we do and let clients decide.

22. We are direct with clients. If they are about to make a mistake, we say so. If a client is wrong, we tell them. This sometimes creates discomfort. That discomfort is part of the value.

23. We do not make guarantees we cannot keep. We do not promise outcomes we cannot control. We promise effort, expertise, and good faith.

24. We do not work with clients we do not trust. If something feels wrong about a prospective engagement, we walk away.

25. We protect client confidentiality absolutely. Client data, infrastructure, and business information do not leave our internal systems unless explicitly required for the work.

26. We do not use client infrastructure or data for our own purposes, training, or demonstration without explicit permission.

---

## Red Lines (Non-Negotiable)

27. We do not work with clients involved in illegal activity, fraud, or intentional misrepresentation to their own customers or regulators.

28. We do not compromise security for convenience. If a client demands we disable MFA, skip encryption, or weaken access controls, we decline the engagement.

29. We do not take on work we do not have capacity to do well. Over-promising to win an engagement is a guarantee of failure.

30. We do not work with clients who treat our team disrespectfully. This includes yelling, threats, sexist or racist language, and treating us as subservient to their internal staff.

---

## Edge Cases We Haven't Resolved

A. We lead with compassion, but only to an extent. We give notice, we offer to pause service, and we try to help them find a solution — but we do not carry debt indefinitely. At some point, the relationship becomes unsustainable and we exit with grace.

B. Yes — as long as there are no legal implications, one meeting can't hurt. A bad-fit referral is still a networking opportunity. We take the meeting, assess honestly, and decline politely if it's not right.

C. Quote it as a learning engagement. Never say no to a potential avenue of revenue. We treat new capability development as a product investment, not a client favor. If the client can pay for us to figure something out, that has value.

D. Yes. When an employee with system access leaves on bad terms, we recommend an immediate access audit. This is part of good ongoing IT hygiene and we flag it proactively.

E. Lead with the hard stop, then a soft explanation. Explain how it will benefit them NOT to violate their own compliance requirements — not why we won't do it, but why they don't want to. We make compliance feel like an advantage, not a burden.
